Traditional Art Styles

Tanjore Painting

Historical Context: Tanjore painting originated in the town of Thanjavur (Tanjore), Tamil Nadu, during the Maratha rule in the 16th century. It was patronized by the Nayakas and Marathas who ruled over the region. Tanjore paintings are known for their rich colors, surface richness, and compact composition.


Materials and Tools:

  • Canvas: Tanjore paintings are typically done on wooden panels or cloth stretched over a wooden base.
  • Paint: Artists use natural pigments and gold foil to create vibrant and rich colors. Traditional pigments include mineral colors, organic dyes, and vegetable colors.
  • Gesso: Gesso made from chalk powder and Arabic gum is applied on the canvas to create a smooth surface for painting.

Technique:

  1. Outline: Tanjore paintings start with drawing the outline of the subject using bold, thick lines.
  2. Base Colors: Once the outline is complete, base colors are applied to the figures using flat brushes.
  3. Gold Foil: One of the distinctive features of Tanjore painting is the use of gold foil to highlight certain elements of the composition, such as ornaments and attire.
  4. Detailing: After applying base colors and gold foil, intricate detailing is done using fine brushes. This includes adding facial features, jewelry, and intricate patterns.

Symbolism and Themes:

  • Tanjore paintings often depict Hindu gods and goddesses, saints, and mythological scenes.
  • Common themes include scenes from Hindu epics like Ramayana and Mahabharata, as well as divine manifestations.

Pattachitra Painting

Historical Context: Pattachitra painting originated in the eastern Indian states of Odisha and West Bengal. The term 'Pattachitra' comes from the Sanskrit words 'patta' (cloth) and 'chitra' (painting). These paintings are known for their intricate details, vibrant colors, and traditional themes.


Materials and Tools:

  • Canvas: Pattachitra paintings are typically done on cloth or dried palm leaves.
  • Paint: Artists use natural pigments derived from minerals, vegetables, and other organic sources. Colors are prepared by grinding natural materials with water or gum arabic.
  • Brushes: Fine brushes made from animal hair or bamboo sticks are used for detailing.

Technique:

  1. Outline: Pattachitra paintings start with drawing the outline of the composition using a thin brush dipped in black paint.
  2. Base Colors: Once the outline is complete, base colors are applied to different elements of the composition.
  3. Detailing: Intricate details are added using fine brushes. This includes adding facial features, clothing patterns, jewelry, and decorative elements.
  4. Narrative Depiction: Pattachitra paintings often depict mythological stories, religious themes, and scenes from everyday life. These narratives are conveyed through detailed imagery and symbolism.

Symbolism and Themes:

  • Pattachitra paintings often depict themes from Hindu mythology, such as stories of gods and goddesses, epic tales, and religious rituals.
  • Common motifs include figures of gods and goddesses, celestial beings, animals, birds, and nature elements.
